## Top 5 Most Critical Assets

### #1: ehr-db-01 — EHR Database Server (PostgreSQL)

This is the single most critical asset in the MedDefense environment. The PostgreSQL database on ehr-db-01 is the authoritative store of protected health information for every patient treated across all three sites — medical histories, medication lists, allergy information, lab results, clinical notes, and treatment plans. A breach of this system triggers mandatory regulatory notification under HIPAA Breach Notification Rule (45 CFR §§164.400-414), with penalties reaching $1.5 million per violation category and potential criminal charges. The database is currently accessible from the entire 10.10.0.0/16 network (confirmed by both Marcus's notes and the network scan), meaning any compromised system can directly query patient records. A single SQL injection or stolen credential could exfiltrate the entire patient population's health records in minutes. If the database is encrypted by ransomware, clinical operations across the entire hospital halt until recovery — and the backup is on a NAS in the same rack, same network, same room. No other asset combines this level of regulatory liability, patient safety impact, and current vulnerability.

### #2: ad-dc-01 — Primary Domain Controller

Active Directory is the keystone of MedDefense's identity infrastructure. Every authentication decision, every access control check, and every security policy enforcement flows through the domain controllers. If an attacker gains administrative access to ad-dc-01, they own the entire organization — every workstation, every server, every badge reader connected to AD, and every service account that runs critical applications. They can create stealth accounts, modify group memberships, change password policies, and deploy malicious group policies to hundreds of machines simultaneously. The domain controller also stores password hashes for all 2,000 employees, which can be extracted and cracked offline. Critically, ad-dc-02 (the secondary DC) is not backed up per the Veeam configuration, meaning if ad-dc-01 is destroyed or corrupted, the only recovery path is rebuilding AD from scratch — a process that could take days or weeks during which no one can log in to any system. In cybersecurity terms, "owning the DC" is synonymous with "owning the organization."

### #3: BD Alaris Infusion Pump Fleet (120 units, firmware 12.1.2)

While individual infusion pumps may seem less significant than servers or databases, their aggregate criticality is exceptional because they directly deliver medication to patients. The BD Alaris pumps run firmware version 12.1.2, which has known vulnerabilities documented in a vendor security bulletin issued approximately 18 months ago. The vendor recommended network isolation as a mitigation — advice that has not been followed. These pumps are connected to the flat 10.10.0.0/16 network, meaning an attacker who compromises any system (billing server, workstation, or even the Netgear router at Westside) can reach them directly. An integrity compromise of infusion pump configurations could alter medication delivery rates — speeding up or slowing down drug administration without clinical staff awareness. This is not a theoretical data breach scenario; this is a potential patient harm scenario. A pump delivering heparin at 10x the prescribed rate, or insulin at a fraction of the intended dose, could kill a patient within minutes. No other asset class in MedDefense's inventory has this direct a path from cyber compromise to patient death.

### #4: billing-srv-01 — Billing/Claims Processing Server

This server earns its position not because billing is more important than patient care, but because it is currently compromised and demonstrates the organization's inability to detect and respond. The cryptominer has been running for 14+ days, consuming 96% of server CPU, communicating with three external mining pools, and no one noticed until a sysadmin filed a performance ticket. The server runs Ubuntu 18.04 (approaching EOL) with Apache 2.4.29, which has known RCE vulnerabilities that were exploited at least twice — first for ransomware in January, now for cryptomining. The attacker has an active foothold on the flat network with potential access to EHR, PACS, and domain controllers. This server is not just a critical billing system — it is a live, confirmed security breach that remains uncontained. Every day it remains on the network is another day the attacker can pivot, escalate, and exfiltrate. It also serves as a case study for the Board: this is what happens when a security incident is treated as a performance problem and root causes are never addressed.

### #5: FortiGate 100F — Perimeter Firewall

The FortiGate is MedDefense's only network security device and the sole boundary between the internet and the internal network. Its ruleset currently allows all services from VPN connections (Rules 2 and 3), permits all outbound traffic with no egress filtering (Rule 4), and the Westside VPN terminates on a consumer-grade Netgear router that could be compromised trivially. If the FortiGate itself is compromised or misconfigured, there is no secondary control — no IDS/IPS, no network segmentation, no deception technology. Every internal asset is exposed. The firewall is also the VPN concentrator for both Westside and HQ; its failure isolates those sites from Central, cutting off EHR access, email, and all shared services. Despite its criticality, the firewall configuration has not been audited by anyone with security expertise — Marcus flagged the permissive rules but never had time to remediate them. The FortiGate represents both MedDefense's strongest control and its biggest single point of failure.
